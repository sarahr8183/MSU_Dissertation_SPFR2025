\chapter{Methods of Sample Characterization}
\label{chMethods}
\indent

This chapter will describe methods of characterization for diamond samples. Because diamond is not thermodynamically favorable at room temperature and pressure, synthetic diamond growth is not straight forward. Thorough characterization of dependent variables is essential in identifying cause-and-effect relationships in the complex system of independent variables that is CVD synthetic diamond growth. First, a discussion of two different methods for measuring the growth rate. Next, methods for characterizing the surface, such as surface roughness via atomic force microscopy (AFM), surface orientation via x-ray diffraction (XRD), and optical microscopy, of the diamond substrate and grown film will be discussed. Lastly, methods for characterizing defect concentrations using Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, Ultra-violet and Visible (UV-Vis) spectroscopy, Raman spectroscopy, and photoluminescent spectroscopy.

\section{Growth Rate}
\label{growthrate}
\indent

Growth rate is a valuable dependent variable in synthetic diamond growth. CVD diamond growth occurs primarily in one direction, normal to the diamond seed surface, whereas HPHT diamond growth occurs in all crystalline directions simultaneously. To determine one directional, vertical, growth rate, a linear encoder is used to calculate the thickness before and after growth. To determine total growth rate, the mass of the sample, before and after growth is measured.

\subsection{Vertical Growth Rate via Linear Encoder}
\label{thickness}
\indent

Vertical growth rate was determined by thickness measurements before and after deposition. To determine sample thickness, a measurement was taken at each corner of the sample and at the center of the sample. At least four measurements were taken at each corner. At least nine measurements were taken at the center of each sample. If the standard deviation of the measurements was greater than 0.001, additional measurements were taken until a maximum standard deviation of 0.001 was achieved. Standard error was calculated for each measurement to determine the spread of sample means around the population mean. Thickness measurements were taken at the center at each corner of the sample using a Mitutoyo EH-10P linear encoder. The difference between the thickness after deposition ($z_{final}$) and before deposition ($z_{inital}$) was then divided by the length of deposition ($t_{deposition}$) determined the vertical growth rate, as shown in equation \ref{vertGR}.

\begin{equation}
\label{vertGR}
    Growth Rate_z = \frac{z_{final} - z_{inital}}{t_{deposition}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Total Growth Rate via Mass Balance}
\label{mass}
\indent

Homoepitaxial diamond growth does not only occur in the vertical direction. Lateral outgrowth is also possible. To account for this, the mass of the substrate before deposition and the mass of the substrate and growth layer, combined, after deposition was collected and used to calculate a total volumetric growth rate of the sample. The sample mass was taken using a Mettler Toledo XS105 mass balance. 

\begin{equation}
\label{vertGR}
    Total Growth Rate = \frac{m_{final} - m_{inital}}{t_{deposition}}
\end{equation}

The total growth rate can be converted to a vertical growth rate for comparison. To do this, the surface area, measured with optical microscopy, for both the starting substrate and the grown film must be determined. The density of diamond can be calculated using known constants such as diamond lattice parameter (a), number of atoms in a diamond unit cell, Avogadro's number, and the molar mass of carbon and Equation \ref{densityDia}. Mass divided by density equals volume. The volume can then be divided by surface area to determine the vertical growth rate, as shown in Equation \ref{totalToVertGR}. This method is more error prone than the method described in Section \ref{thickness} because the measurement software on the optical microscope used to measure surface area is not calibrated. Mass data was collected for all samples, however, reported growth rates were measured using the  technique in Section \ref{thickness}.

\begin{equation}
\label{densityDia}
    \rho_{diamond} = (\frac{8 atom}{unit cell})(\frac{unit cell}{(a= 0.367 nm)^3})(\frac{1 mol}{6.022 * 10^{23} atoms})(\frac{12.011 g}{1 mol})
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{totalToVertGR}
    Growth Rate_z= \frac{(m_{final}A_{final})-(m_{inital}A_{initial})}{\rho_{diamond} *t_{deposition}}
\end{equation}

\section{Surface Condition}
\label{sectionSurfaceCondition}
\indent

There are several surface condition variables that influence synthetic diamond growth. Surface condition can also be used to asses the quality of a deposition. Three methods for evaluating the surface condition will be described in the following section. First, optical microscopy is used to identify micron-scale features, such as large polishing features or unepitaxial crystallites, identify growth morphology on grown films, and determine the presence and relative intensity of strain within a substrate. Second, atomic force microscopy (AFM) is used to identify nanometer-scale features and quantify surface roughness. Third, x-ray diffraction (XRD) is used to determine the surface orientation and offcut.

\subsection{Optical Microscopy}
\subsubsection{Differential Interference Contrast Microscopy}
\label{DICM}
\indent

Differential interference contrast microscopy (DICM) is an optical microscopy technique that utilizes properties of polarized light to improve the resolution of features in optical microscopy. This work utilized a Nikon ECLIPSE ME600 microscope. Surface features such as polishing lines, etch pits, and step flow growth can be observed using this technique.

Growth morphology is a valuable characterization technique for crystal growth. Nucleation and propagation styles can be inferred from growth morphology. Variations in nucleation and propagation styles can affect the strain within the grown film. For example, nucleation that occurs at step edges and progresses linearly along the step edge until a complete new step is formed produces a growth morphology called step flow growth and indicates low strain in the grown film due to the orderly nucleation and propagation \cite{Frenklach1996SurfaceGrowth, Sunagawa1999GrowthCrystals, Burton1951THECRYSTALS, YagiStepSurfaces}. An alternative growth morphology results from nucleation that occurs on flat planes, not at step edges. The nucleation sites initiate growth that results in a number of "islands" growing in size until they encounter a neighboring island. The resulting morphology resembles that of an orange peel with numerous bumps growing into one another. This growth morphology is called orange peel, due to its appearance, and is associated with lower quality, higher strain growth due to the disorder in nucleation and propagation. 

Deposition temperature affects growth morphology. If deposition temperatures are too low, adsorbing atoms do not have sufficient energy to move about the surface to achieve the lowest energy adsorption site, a step edge, creating nucleation sites on the flat planes. The island-like nucleation sites propagate in any direction. This produced a common CVD defect known as unepitaxial crystallites \cite{Tallaire2008OriginFilms}. Unpeitaxial crystallites are isolated defect regions of a different orientation than the substrate. Unepitaxial crystallites often appear dark in color, as compared to the bulk substrate and grown film, because light scatters off these defects. If deposition temperatures are too high, however, such that the growth rate is sufficiently high, nucleation sites begin to propagate before adatoms can move to step edges resulting in numerous crystal orientations growing in the vertical direction, rather than the crystallite in the preferred direction out competing unfavorable crystallites, which can produce polycrystalline diamond films \cite{VanDerDrift1967EVOLUTIONARYLAYERS}. Addition of nitrogen in the gas phase is another way to increase growth rate, however, increased growth rate via nitrogen can also lead to polycrystalline diamond growth and is undesirable for many end uses, including quantum applications \cite{Cao1996GrowthNitrogen}.


\subsubsection{Birefringence}
\label{Birefringence}
\indent

Birefringence is a technique used to qualitative determine strain in a sample \cite{Raman1944BirefringenceDiamond,Lang1967CausesDiamond, ProfTolansky1964BirefringenceDiamond}. Strain in can arise from a number of sources. Sources of strain in diamond include: dislocations, lattice parameter variation, precipitates and inclusions, fractures, and plastic deformation \cite{Lang1967CausesDiamond}. The presence of internal strain causes local changes in refractive index which is the source of birefringence \cite{Atkins1968QuantumRotation}.

To measure birefringence, the substrate is placed between two cross polarizers. If the SCD is completely strain free, the optically isotropic crystal will not have an effect on the light and therefore no light will pass through. In the presence of strain, the polarization of the light incident on the substrate will rotate allowing some light to pass through the second polarizer. The amount of internal strain can be qualified by the intensity of the light. In other words, the brighter the region, the more strain present.

\subsection{Atomic Force Microscopy}
\label{AFM}
\indent 

Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) was used to determine the surface roughness of the substrates. Three 10$\mu$m by 10$\mu$m AFM scans were taken at different locations on the sample. The areal average roughness (SA) was calculated for each scan and the average of the three scans was reported. In this work, a Hitachi AFM5100N
atomic force microscope equipped with a MikroMasch
HQ:NSC14/Al tip and operating in non-contact mode was used.

Atomic force microscopy was invented by Binnig et. al. in 1986 \cite{BinnigAtomicMicroscope}. The AFM technology drew principles from profilometry and scanning tunneling microscopy to resolve atomic scale features using cantilever arm with an atomically fine tip. The cantilever with an atomically fine tip tracks across the surface producing a two-dimensional profile of vertical height (change in z if considering a Cartesian coordinate system) difference across the horizontal distance traveled (x or y in a Cartesian coordinate system). The atomic scale features include ridges from polishing, morphological features, etch pits, nanofabricated structures, etc. The size, depth, and frequency of features are all elements that contribute to surface roughness. Surface roughness can be calculated in several different ways. Two common ways include average roughness (R\textsubscript{a}) and ten-point height (R\textsubscript{z}). Average roughness reports the average deviation from the mean line over a sampling length, see Equation \ref{RaEQN} \cite{Thomas1981CharacterizationRoughness, Nayak1973SomeMeasurement}. An example of average roughness is shown in Figure \ref{fig:RaGraph}.

\begin{equation}
    \label{RaEQN}
    R_a=\frac{1}{L}\int^L|z|dx
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/Ra.png}
    \caption{A graphical representation of R\textsubscript{a} surface roughness.}
    \label{fig:RaGraph}
\end{figure}

The second common way to calculate surface roughness is ten-point height, a metric commonly used if extreme height changes are critical to performance. Ten-point height is the average of the difference between the five highest peaks and the five lowest valleys in a sampling length, see Equation \ref{RzEQN} \cite{Thomas1981CharacterizationRoughness}.

\begin{equation}
    \label{RzEQN}
    R_z=(\sum_{i=1}^5P_i-\sum_{i=1}^5V_i)/5
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/Rz.png}
    \caption{A graphical representation of R\textsubscript{z} surface roughness.}
    \label{fig:RzGraph}
\end{figure}

The roughness metrics R\textsubscript{a} and R\textsubscript{z} characterize roughness across one-dimension. One metric for two-dimensional roughness characterization measures the average spacing between local peaks called S\textsubscript{a} \cite{Gadelmawla2002RoughnessParameters}. The equation used to calculate the mean spacing between local peaks is shown in Equation \ref{SaEQN}. This measurement of surface roughness provides a better description surface irregularities. For the remainder of this work, mean spacing between local peaks (S\textsubscript{a}) will be referred to as surface roughness.

\begin{equation}
    \label{SaEQN}
    S_a=\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N|z_n-z_{ave}|}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{figures/SameRoughnessComparison.png}
    \caption{Two different surfaces morphologies with equivalent surface roughness metrics.}
    \label{fig:RoughnessComparison}
\end{figure}

It is important to note that surface roughness values only do not provide a comprehensive description of the surface. For example, the two surfaces shown in Figure \ref{fig:RoughnessComparison} have the same measured roughness but exhibit significantly different features. This observation and the results of the experimental series that will be discussed in Chapter \ref{chPressureOffcut} led to the investigation of surface condition normalization, as will be discussed in Chapter \ref{chSurfacePrep}.

Polished diamond surfaces can be characterized using AFM to calculate surface roughness \cite{Hardy2024ChemicalApplications}. Surface roughness results were calculated using Gwyddion, a free program for scanning probe data analysis and visualization. Average spacing between local peaks surface roughness is used to evaluate the quality of the polished diamond surface. Typically, a good polish is considered to have a surface roughness of not more than 1 nm.


\subsection{Surface Orientation}
\label{XRD}
\indent

The substrate surface orientation can affect growth morphology and defect incorporation \cite{Burns1990GROWTH-SECTORDIAMONDS,Ushizawa1998BoronDiamond,Takeuchi2000HomoepitaxialSubstrates}. A Rigaku Smartlab X-ray Diffractometer (XRD) was used to measure single crystal diamond surface orientation. X-ray diffraction is a technique that uses electromagnetic radiation of x-ray wavelengths ($\lambda$ = 10$^9$ - 10$^{12}$ m) to measure the spacing between crystal planes through constructive and destructive interference patterns of x-rays reflected off of atoms in an ordered structure. Bragg's Law, shown in equation \ref{braggslaw}, is the foundation of this technique where \textit{n} is the diffraction order, $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the x-rays being used (Cu K$\alpha_2$ = 1.54 \AA), \textit{d} is the spacing between crystal planes, and $\theta$ describes the angle at which constructive interference occurs and is called the Bragg angle \cite{BraggTheCrystals,Maskil1988StructureLine}.

\begin{equation}
\label{braggslaw}
    n\lambda=2dsin\theta
\end{equation}

An x-ray diffractometer has an incident side and a detector side. In the Rigaku Smartlab, the sample stage rotates about its center, $\phi$ axis, and the incident side and detector side rotate about the sample stage, $\omega$ and 2$\theta$ axes. It can be observed from the configuration, as shown in figure \ref{fig:XRDConfig}, that $\omega$ and $\theta$ are congruent. If the lattice parameter of the unit cell is known but the surface orientation of a single crystal sample is not known, a scan of the 2$\theta$ axis can be performed. When constructive interference occurs, a peak in detected intensity will be observed which can be used to determine the Bragg angle. 

\begin{equation}
    \label{interplanarspacing}
    d=\frac{a}{\sqrt(h^2+k^2+l^2)}
\end{equation}

The distance between crystal planes, in \textit{d}, can be calculated from the Bragg angle. Then, the Miller indices (\textit{h, k, l}) can be calculated from the interplanar spacing using equation \ref{interplanarspacing} and allowed reflection rules. 

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{figures/XRD_axes.png}
    \caption{The figure defines relevant measurement axes for x-ray diffraction offcut measurements.}
    \label{fig:XRDConfig}
\end{figure}

In addition to using XRD to confirm the surface orientation, XRD can be used to measure the angle at which the physical surface of the crystal is off with respect to the intended orientation \cite{Bragg1968X-RAYCRYSTALLOGRAPHY}. This angle is called the offcut angle, miscut angle, or misorientation angle. To measure the offcut, 2$\theta$ is fixed at the expected value for the surface orientation for the single crystal sample. If the physical surface of the sample is precisely aligned with the crystal orientation, $\theta$ and $\omega$ will be equivalent to exactly half of 2$\theta$. If the physical surface of the sample is misoriented from the crystal structure, $\omega$ will not equate to half of 2$\theta$ and the offcut can be calculated using equation \ref{offcuteqn}.

\begin{equation}
\label{offcuteqn}
    offcut = \frac{(\omega_{\phi=x}-\omega_{\phi=x+180^\circ})}{2}
\end{equation}

To take an offcut measurement, with 2$\theta$ fixed an $\omega$ scan can be taken on the center of the single crystal sample for various rotations of $\phi$. Half of the difference between the maximum $\omega$ and the minimum $\omega$, which should be 180$^\circ$ apart, is equivalent to the offcut. To promote step-flow growth in chemical vapor deposition of single crystal diamond, an offcut of greater than 3$^\circ$ with respect to the (100) plane is desired \cite{Lee1997AFilms}.

\section{Ultra-violet and Visible Spectroscopy}
\label{UVVis}
\indent

The next technique used to characterize defects was Ultra-Violet and Visible (UV-Vis) Spectroscopy on a Shimadzu UV-3600 UV-VIS-NIR Spectrophotometer with integrating sphere attachment. The integrating sphere is an attachment often used for samples with high surface reflections or internal scattering to achieve high efficiency and low error without the need for a photomultiplier tube \cite{Castiglioni2000AnSamples,Jacquez1955TheorySphere}. Ultra-violet and visible spectroscopy is useful in diamond characterization because many commonly found defects in diamond absorb in ultra-violet and visible wavelengths. In fact, crystalline defects absorbing visible wavelengths in the otherwise transparent diamond host is the reason some diamonds appear colored \cite{Collins2003TheSpectroscopy,Fisher2009BrownDefects, Gaillou2010SpectroscopicDiamonds} . For example, vacancy clusters absorb at 360 nm \cite{Jones2009DislocationsDiamond},  NVH\textsuperscript{0} defects absorb at 520 nm \cite{Khan2013Colour-causingDiamond}, and neutral single substitutional nitrogen (N\textsubscript{S}\textsuperscript{0}) defects absorb at 270 nm \cite{Chrenko1971DispersedDiamonds}. These defects are particularly interesting because they do not overlap with the broad absorption features  other common defects, the negative (NV\textsuperscript{-}) and neutral (NV\textsuperscript{0}) nitrogen vacancy defects, produce when characterizing via UV-Vis. The negative (NV\textsuperscript{-}) and neutral (NV\textsuperscript{0}) nitrogen vacancy defects can be characterized using other techniques, such as Photoluminescent spectroscopy, and will be described in more detail in Section \ref{PL}.

A typical ultra-violet and visible spectrum of an ultra pure single crystal diamond with very low defect concentrations shows low or no absorption at ultra-violet and visible wavelength below the band gap energy.  An example of a UV-Vis spectrum is shown in Figure \ref{fig:ELSCDUVVis}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includesvg[width=1\linewidth]{figures/20241202-UVVis-EL-TLuo.svg}
    \caption{Ultra-violet and visible absorption spectrum of an electronic grade single crystal diamond.}
    \label{fig:ELSCDUVVis}
\end{figure}

Ultra-violet and visible spectroscopy has been used to quantify low concentrations of N\textsubscript{s}\textsuperscript{0} in single crystal diamond by fitting the UV-Vis spectrum and correlating the concentrations of N\textsubscript{s}\textsuperscript{0} with the intensity of the 270 nm absorption peak \cite{Luo2022RapidSpectroscopy}. To improve the accuracy of the fit, Luo et. al. described a method where five distinct functions are summed to fit a sample's UV-Vis spectrum \cite{Luo2022RapidSpectroscopy}. The first distinct function is a "ramp" function that decreases as the inverse of wavelength cubed increases which defines the general profile of the spectrum \cite{Khan2013Colour-causingDiamond}. The next function is an electronic grade diamond, which is essentially defect free, offset as shown in \ref{fig:ELSCDUVVis} \cite{Luo2022RapidSpectroscopy}. Finally, three Gaussian functions centered at 270 nm, 360 nm, and 520 nm, respectively, are fit corresponding to the three distinct defects described above \cite{Luo2022RapidSpectroscopy}. This method shows success for quantifying low concentrations of N\textsubscript{s}\textsuperscript{0}, however, it performs poorly for higher concentrations, greater than a few tenths of ppm \cite{Chrenko1971DispersedDiamonds}.

\section{Raman Spectroscopy}
\label{Raman}
\indent

Raman spectroscopy is a non-destructive method to differentiate allotropes of carbon, diamond versus graphite, through probing bonding structure, crystallinity, and disorder \cite{Knight1989CharacterizationSpectroscopy, Shroder1990AnalysisSpectroscopyb}. Raman spectroscopy is fundamentally the use of Raman scattering to understand differences in bonding configurations due to lattice vibrations. The first order diamond, \textit{sp\textsuperscript{3}} carbon, Raman line occurs at 1332 cm\textsuperscript{-1}. Graphitic carbon, \textit{sp\textsuperscript{2}}, has two bands that correspond to different vibrational modes. The first band, called the D band, occurs at 1350 cm\textsuperscript{-1}. The D band is associated with disorder in the graphitic structure. The second bad, called the G band, occurs at 1580 cm\textsuperscript{-1}. Graphitic carbon produces a more intense Raman spectrum. This allows diamond researchers to detect a small amount of graphite in diamond films. 

Raman spectra were originally taken on a Horiba LabRAM ARAMIS Raman spectrometer that uses 532 nm excitation. After the completion of the QuOD optics lab, Raman data was extracted from the photoluminescent data using methods described in Section \ref{PL} and on the experimental setup shown in Figure \ref{fig:PLsetup} which was equiped with 532 nm excitation and 693 nm excitation. 

\section{Photoluminescence Spectroscopy}
\label{PL}
\indent

Photoluminescent spectroscopy was used to qualitatively compare the quantity of defects, such as the nitrogen vacancy defect and silicon vacancy defect \cite{Collins1992TheSpectroscopy}. Each defect has a unique luminescence based on its electronic structure, so long as it exists within the band gap of the host material. In this technique, the sample is illuminated using a chosen excitation wavelength. The excitation wavelength should be less than the band gap to prevent production of electron hole pairs. Additionally, the excitation wavelength may be strategically chosen to selectively excite some defects while not providing sufficient energy to excite others. A spectrum is taken on the luminescence of the sample. Each defect will luminesce at a wavelength equivalent to the energy difference between the excited state and the ground state; This luminescence is called the zero-phonon line. The zero-phonon line for NV\textsuperscript{-} and NV\textsuperscript{0} are 637 nm and 575 nm, respectively \cite{Fraczek2017LaserDiamond}. A schematic of the home-built experimental setup used in this work is shown in Figure \ref{fig:PLsetup}. The setup was later modified to accommodate two different excitation wavelengths (532 nm and 695 nm) with largely the same setup. The excitation pathway was switched by strategic flip mirror placement.

\section{Electron Paramagnetic Resonance}
\label{EPR}
\indent

Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) is a non-destructive method to determine the number of electron spins in a sample which has been used not only to quantify diamond defects but also to validate UV-Vis and FTIR methods to quantify defects \cite{Nadolinny1999EPRNitrogen,Nadolinny2025DefectDiamonds, Loubser1978ElectronDiamond}. Electrons will resonate with different frequencies depending on their surroundings producing a characteristic peak location and splitting depending on the type of defect. For nitrogen related defects in diamond, the magnetic field was swept from 2600 G to 4000 G. The P1 and P2 center, which correspond to substituional nitrogen, in diamond exhibit an EPR signal at 3495 G (g=2.0023) \cite{Hoinkis1991ParamagneticFilms,Smith1959Electron-SpinDiamond,VanWyk1997TheDiamond}. The NV\textsuperscript{-} defect produces an EPR signal in the wings of the P1 and P2 center single \cite{Redman1991SpinSpectroscopy}. In this work, a Bruker EMX-plus spectrometer operating at X-band (9.32 GHz) frequencies was used.

An EPR signal is the first derivative of absorption. To quantify the number of spins contributing to each signal, a double integral over the signal must be computed and converted to spin concentration using a calibration curve, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:BDPAcalibration}. A calibration curve was made using a stable radical with a comparable g-value  $\alpha$,$\gamma$-bisdiphenylene-$\beta$-phenylallyl (BDPA) \cite{Mandal2020OnRadicals}. A serial dilution of BDPA in potassium bromide (KBr), a non-EPR active solid, to create six calibration elements that spanned the expected concentration range.

EPR was used to validated the FTIR method for determining nitrogen concentration described in Section \ref{FTIR}. EPR spectra were taken on four diamond samples. A total spin concentration was calculated using the BDPA calibration curve. This spin value was plotted against a weighted sum of the 1130 cm\textsuperscript{-1}and 1280 cm\textsuperscript{-1} peak area measured with FTIR as shown in Figure \ref{fig:EPRvalidationFTIR}. In Equation \ref{subNconcEq} and Equation \ref{aggNConcEq}, the calibration coefficients K\textsubscript{S2} and K\textsubscript{A2} are the weighting of the respective FTIR peak. This method gives calibration coefficients of K\textsubscript{S2} = 49.54 ppm/cm\textsuperscript{-2} and K\textsubscript{A2} = 51.84 ppm/cm\textsuperscript{-2}.
