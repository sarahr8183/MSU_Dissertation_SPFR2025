\chapter{Effects of Pressure and Offcut on Nitrogen Incorporation}
\label{chPressureOffcut}
\indent

This chapter presents a series of experiments designed to investigate the effects of three key process parameters, temperature, reactor pressure and substrate offcut, on nitrogen incorporation with the goal of identifying specific conditions to favorably incorporate nitrogen as single substitutional nitrogen and suppress the incorporation of nitrogen vacancy defects. Establishing such conditions is a crucial first step in developing layered structures to improve z-control of nitrogen vacancy defect via laser writing, as discussed in Section \ref{idealSubstrate}, which will ultimately improve spatial resolution of diamond quantum sensors with NV centers. After determining the optimal temperature, initial studies showed a correlation between these parameters and substrate surface roughness, suggesting that uncontrolled variation in surface morphology might obscure the underlying relationships.

These findings motivated the investigation into substrate surface roughness, described in detail in Chapter \ref{chSurfacePrep}, which established a surface preparation method to normalize the surface condition. Building on this methodology, the reactor pressure and substrate offcut experiments were repeated with normalized surface roughness as a controlled variable. This approach enabled more precise assessment of how pressure and substrate offcut influence nitrogen incorporation, isolating their effects from the confounding factor of surface roughness.

The results presented in this chapter therefore provide a refined understanding of the effects of process condition on nitrogen incorporation, serving as a foundation for subsequent optimization.

\section{Temperature Optimization}
\indent

To determine the optimized substrate temperature during deposition, three 3.5 mm x 3.5 mm x 1 mm (100) HPHT substrates from Sumitomo were laser cut to approximately 3$^{\circ}$ offcut orientation. The top surfaces were mechanically polished. Each sample was examined using DICM to record the initial surface condition, substrate thickness was measured using a linear encoder, and surface orientation was measured using XRD. Next, a deposition at different temperatures spanning the stable operation region was performed on each sample. The total gas flow rate for deposition was 433.5 sccm of hydrogen (H$_2$), 16 sccm of methane (CH$_4$), and 17.5 sccm of 0.01 mol\% nitrogen (N$_2$) in hydrogen. This provided a gas phase with 3.69\% methane and 48 ppm of nitrogen. A 10-minute hydrogen etch was performed at the start of each deposition. At the conclusion of the etch, the nitrogen in hydrogen was introduced first followed by the methane. Each deposition ran for 2.5 hours. The microwave power was shut off promptly to end the deposition and prevent nucleation of defects due to continued deposition. These three samples were grown at a constant pressure of 290 mbar and three different temperatures of 950 $^{\circ}$C, 1000 $^{\circ}$C, and 1050 $^{\circ}$C. The microwave power was adjusted throughout the deposition to maintain the substrate temperature to within ±10 $^{\circ}$C of the chosen temperature to determine temperature effects on sample morphology. Due to the high nitrogen concentration in the HPHT substrates, no spectroscopy was performed to analyze the nitrogen incorporation for this study.

DICM was used to evaluate the sample morphology. Ordered orange peel morphology with some scallop features was observed on the deposition that occurred at 1050$^\circ$C. Scallop morphology with underlying step flow growth was observed on 1000$^\circ$C. Pure step flow growth was observed on the sample growth at 950$^\circ$C. The DICM results for the three temperatures tested are shown in Figure \ref{fig: TempOptMorph}. The highest temperature gave the most significant orange peel morphology while the lowest temperature (950 $^{\circ}$C) exhibited the best step flow morphology. Based on these results, 950 $^{\circ}$C was selected as the substrate temperature for the next set of depositions.


\section{Influence of Reactor Pressure on Nitrogen Incorporation}
\label{pressureSeries}
\indent

After determining the optimized temperature of 950 $^{\circ}$C, eight 3 mm x 3 mm x 0.25 mm (100) CVD substrates (MTI) were pre-characterized and cleaned, as described above, in the as-received condition. These samples were used to for two separate studies. First, to determine the effects of pressure on four samples all with approximately 1.5$^{\circ}$ offcuts. This series is called pressure trials or initial pressure series. The second used three samples at lower offcuts, approximately 0.5$^\circ$, to verify the previous results and one sample at a higher offcut than the initial series of approximately 2$^{\circ}$ offcut. This series is called offcut series or offcut trials. As these substrates were CVD substrates, the nitrogen background was low and therefore spectroscopic characterization could be performed to determine nitrogen incorporation in the grown film. In the pressure series, the first deposition performed was at 290 mbar and 950$^\circ$C to duplicate the results from the temperature optimization series with added spectroscopic characterization. The total gas flow rate during deposition for these trials was reduced while keeping the ratios constant to fix the total flow rate at 400 sccm. Therefore, the flow rates were as follows: 368 sccm of H$_2$, 16 sccm of CH$_4$, and 16.5 sccm of 0.01 mol\% N$_2$ in H$_2$ giving total gas phase composition of 4\% carbon and 50 ppm of nitrogen. The 10-minute hydrogen etch was performed for each of these samples as well.
	
The pressure series of depositions started with sample MTI21\_20a. The MTI21\_20a deposition pressure and temperature of 950 $^{\circ}$C and 290 mbar were chosen to replicate the conditions found to be ideal in the previous temperature series. The pressure was increased for subsequent growths, i.e. MTI21\_14a at 330 mbar followed by MTI21\_15a at 370 mbar, and the final sample, MTI21\_08a, was grown at the lowest pressure of 250 mbar to conclude the first pressure series trial. Orange peel growth was observed on MTI21\_20a and MTI21\_14a while MTI21\_08a and MTI21\_15a, which were grown at the extreme pressures of the study, showed good step flow growth. Morphology results for the inital pressure series are shown in Table \ref{PressureMorphology} Unepitaxial crystallites were present on samples MTI21\_14a and MTI21\_15a, thus, leading to the conclusion that the ideal conditions for deposition, based on morphology alone, were 950 $^o$C and 250 mbar.

Several spectroscopic analysis techniques were used to characterize the defect incorporation in each sample. FTIR spectra were taken before and after deposition to observe differences in the spectra after nitrogen doped growth on the pressure series. FTIR was not taken on samples for the temperature series as the HPHT substrate background nitrogen content would have dominated the results. As mentioned previously, the first sample growth for this series was MTI21\_20a at the same conditions as the ideal conditions selected from the temperature series (950 $^{\circ}$ and 290 mbar), then pressure was increased incrementally for subsequent substrates and a low pressure concluded the series. Results are shown in Figure \ref{fig: FTIRInitial}. Defined peaks at approximately 1330 cm$^{-1}$ and 2930 cm$^{-1}$ were visible on the first sample grown, MTI21\_20a, and decreased in intensity for subsequent growths. This brought about suspicion of contamination in the system, possibly due to the new sample holder or quartz dome.

Of the initial pressure series samples, MTI21\_20a (290 mbar) appeared darkest in color, followed by MTI21\_14a (330 mbar). Sample MTI21\_20a (290 mbar) exhibited the greatest absorption followed by MTI21\_14a (330 mbar), consistent with naked-eye observations. The spectra of samples MTI21\_15a (370 mbar) and MTI21\_15a (250 mbar) appeared like those of pure diamond, with very little absorption in the UV-Vis region.

\section{Substrate Orientation Dependence on Nitrogen Related Diamond Defects}
\label{offcutSeries}
\indent

Because of contamination suspicion discussed in Section \ref{pressureSeries}, the repetition of some of the conditions (250 mbar, 290 mbar, and 370 mbar) was necessary. The samples chosen for these repeats were MTI21\_19a, MTI21\_10a, and MTI21\_11a. These samples were of a lower offcut than the initial samples. The deposition conditions of the second was identical to the previous series. The total gas flow rate during deposition was 400 sccm. The flow rates of each gas were as follows: 368 sccm of H$_2$, 16 sccm of CH$_4$, and 16.5 sccm of 0.01 mol\% N$_2$ in H$_2$ giving total gas phase composition of 4\% carbon and 50 ppm of nitrogen. A 10-minute hydrogen etch was performed prior to deposition for each of these samples. All three of these samples exhibited an orange peel morphology which seems to indicate that a lower offcut results in a poor growth morphology. To substantiate this, one final deposition was done at 950 $^{\circ}$C and 250 mbar on sample MTI21\_09a, which was at a higher offcut of approximately 2$^{\circ}$ with respect to the (100) surface. Sample MTI21\_09a exhibited orange peel morphology indicating that the offcut of the sample does not correlate with growth morphology. Morphological results are shown in Table \ref{PressureMorphology}.

A second series of growths, at the same conditions as the previous four but with lower offcuts, were completed to investigate the potential contamination. These results are shown in Figure \ref{fig: FTIRLowOffcut}. Not only did the 1330 cm$^{-1}$ and 2930 cm$^{-1}$ peaks persist, but the intensity of each peak was also greater than in the initial pressure series, suggesting a dependence on offcut. Positive substitutional nitrogen N$_Z^+$ is correlated with an FTIR absorption peak at 1332 cm\textsuperscript{-1}. One additional sample was grown at 250 mbar and 950 $^{\circ}$C with an offcut of 2$^{\circ}$ with respect to the (100) surface, greater than the second samples grown at the same condition, to study the effects of offcut on the FTIR spectrum. These results are shown in Figure \ref{fig: FTIR250}. The results of this final deposition did not indicate a correlation between offcut and the intensity of the 1330 cm$^{-1}$ and 2930 cm$^{-1}$ peaks.

\section{Confounding Variables Affecting Nitrogen Incorporation}
\indent

The morphological and spectroscopic results of the inital pressure series and the offcut series which intended to study the effects of pressure and offcut on the quality of growth and nitrogen incorporation were inconclusive. No correlations between pressure nor offcut with morphology, growth rate, FTIR spectroscopy relevant peak intensity, UVVis spectroscopy total absorption, UVVis defect specific peak absorption, or Raman spectroscopy were observed. This prompted further investigation into other variables not strictly controlled. Section \ref{RoughnessEffects} discussed the investigation of surface roughness as a confounding variable in the effects of nitrogen incorporation.

\subsection{Effects of Surface Roughness}
\label{RoughnessEffects}
\indent

One variable that was measured but not controlled was substrate surface roughness as measured with atomic force microscopy (AFM), described in Section \ref{AFM}. Surface roughness prior to deposition for all eight samples in the pressure and offcut series are shown in Table \ref{AFMPressure}. When comparing substrate roughness to the metrics discussed in previous sections, the substrates with the lowest surface roughness correlate to the samples with the greatest defect concentration as determined by total UVVis absorption spectra and relevant peak intensity in FTIR spectra, see Figure \ref{fig:RoughnessDependence250mbar} for a comprehensive summary. These results suggest substrate roughness has a significant impact on the deposition layer quality and defect incorporation.

A negative correlation between defect concentration and substrate surface roughness seemed counterintuitive as most substrate surface preparation processes for diamond growth aim to reduce surface roughness to improve deposition quality. However, surface roughness is not an extensive description of a surface. The high surface roughness observed on substrates that resulted in high quality step flow growth and low defect incorporation could have described a surface with many small step edges rather than a surface with a few large, deep polishing lines It was clear additional processing was necessary to normalize the surface condition and isolate the effects of pressure and offcut on nitrogen incorporation. The investigation of additional processing to normalize the surface condition is described in Chapter \ref{chSurfacePrep}.

\section{Dual-Parameter Variation: Reactor Pressure and Substrate Offcut with Normalized Surface Condition}
\label{pressureOffcutRedo}
\indent

Once a process to normalize the substrate surface condition was established, a new pressure and offcut series was complete under the same conditions described in Sections \ref{pressureSeries} and \ref{offcutSeries} but with the addition of the surface normalization process. The results for this series are described in the following sections.

\subsection{Surface Condition Normalization Method}
\label{surfaceNormProcess}
\indent

To isolate the effect of reactor pressure and substrate surface offcut on nitrogen incorporation, a 5 hour hydrogen plasma etch was implemented prior to deposition. A 5 hour hydrogen plasma etch preferentially attacks polishing damage removing amorphous diamond on the substrate surface and exposing pristine crystalline facets for high quality chemical vapor deposition.

\subsection{Efficacy of Substrate Surface Normalization Procedure}
\label{pressureOffcutRedo}
\indent

A second series of depositions with normalized surface condition was completed. Eight 3 mm x 3 mm x 0.25 mm (100) CVD Flawless Technical Diamond (FTD) substrates were mechanically polished to a surface roughness less than or equal to 1 nm then etched in a hydrogen plasma for 5 hours based on the results discussed in Chapter \ref{chSurfacePrep}. The gas phase composition of the normalized surface condition deposition was the same as the pressure series and offcut series described in Section \ref{pressureSeries} and Section \ref{offcutSeries}. The gas phase consisted of 4\% methane and 50 ppm nitrogen resulting in a 1238 ppm N:C. The total gas flow rate was set to 400 sccm. Here, reactor pressure was varied from 250 mbar to 370 mbar in increments of 40 mbar, the same reactor pressure data points explored in Section \ref{pressureSeries}. All four samples in surface normalized pressure series had an offcut of approximately 0.5$^\circ$ with respect to the (100) plane. The effects of offcut on nitrogen incorporation in the surface normalized series was studied at the pressure extremes, 250 mbar and 370 mbar. At 250 mbar and 370 mbar, a deposition on a substrate with an offcut less than 0.5$^\circ$ and greater than 1$^\circ$. A summary of deposition conditions is shown in Table \ref{tab:initialDepoConditions}. DICM results for all eight samples are shown in Figure \ref{fig:PressureOffcutRedoMorphSummary}

PL spectra for each sample was normalized to the Raman line to qualitatively compare the concentrations of NV$^0$, NV$^-$, and SiV$^-$. A strong presence of NV$^{0/-}$ defects are observed in all samples. FTD24\_13a had the lowest relative NV$^{0/-}$ concentration, however, the deposition was PCD as opposed to SCD making it not ideal for quantum sensing application. NV$^0$ is present in all eight samples. N\textsubscript{S} donates an electron to NV$^0$ to form NV$^-$ \cite{Fraczek2017LaserDiamond}. The strong presence of NV$^-$ in the present of NV$^0$ indicates a high concentrations of N\textsubscript{S}. This is further validated based on the concentration of N\textsubscript{S} determined using FITR, as discussed in Section \ref{FTIR}. The nitrogen concentration in the PCD samples was several thousand ppm, these results are shown in Table \ref{tab:depoResults}. SiV$^-$ is observed in all deposition. This is to be expected, despite no intentional silicon doping, because the silicon from the quartz dome is sputtered off and incorporates into the diamond.

To determine the effects of reactor pressure and substrate offcut on nitrogen incorporation, concentrations of various nitrogen related defects (N\textsubscript{S}, NV$^0$, NV$-$, and NVH$^0$) as determined with FTIR, PL, and UV-Vis spectroscopy was plotted against the independent variables (reactor pressure and substrate offcut). No correlation was observed in the nitrogen incorporation and process variables.Therefore, nitrogen incorporation is unresponsive to pressure and offcut variation. Additionally, nitrogen related defects were plotted against growth rate and substrate surface roughness. No correlation was observed in nitrogen incorporation and growth rate nor surface roughness. The lack of correlation with surface roughness indicates the surface preparation established in Chapter \ref{chSurfacePrep} effectively normalized the surface prior to deposition.

This chapter presents an investigation of reactor parameter effects on nitrogen incorporation. Initially, nitrogen incorporation correlated to surface roughness of the substrate. Effects due to surface roughness were removed through the implementation of a surface preparation procedure to normalized the surface. Subsequent results with normalized surface condition show no correlation in nitrogen incorporation with reactor parameters at high nitrogen concentration indicating a low sensitivity due to the reactor pressure and substrate offcut.
