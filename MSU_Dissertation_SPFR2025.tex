\begin{abstract}
The materials engineering challenge limiting the extensive use of the negative nitrogen vacancy defect (NV$^-$) in diamond for high spatial resolution quantum sensing motivates this work. Laser writing of NV$^-$ center shows promise for manufacturing high spatial resolution quantum sensing through precise placement of defects in a two-dimensional array. This technique suffers with control precise placement of the defects in the z-dimension. Materials engineering offers a solution to z-dimension placement by manufacturing ideal substrates of undoped, high-quality single crystal diamond (SCD) with a delta doped nitrogen layer. This work aims to manufacture this ideal substrate through chemical vapor deposition of high-quality diamond and  \textit{in situ} doping. 
Parametric analysis on a bell jar style microwave plasma assisted chemical vapor deposition (MPACVD) reactor to study the effects of reactor pressure, substrate temperature, substrate offcut, and substrate surface preparation on growth quality and defect incorporation using characterization techniques such as Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), Ultra-violet spectroscopy (UV-Vis), Raman spectroscopy, Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM), and Differential Interference Contrast Microscopy (DICM) are discussed. Preliminary results from temperature optimization, pressure optimization, and substrate offcut effects on defect incorporation during \textit{in situ} CVD doping are presented. On-going work to evaluate the effects of substrate preparation on growth quality and defect incorporation is outlined. Additionally, future work to design and build a laminar tube flow CVD reactor to improve spatial control of the delta doped layer is described. The basis for a Ph.D. dissertation will culminate from the completed, on-going, and future work presented.
	
\end{abstract}
